كتاب العبادله
K. al-ʿAbādilah(RG 2)
Probably Damascus, <626
Composition History
Date or place of composition is not known. But it must be before 626H, as there is a samāʿ on Shehit Ali 2826 dated 22nd of Dhū al-Ḥijja 626H.
Incipit
al-ḥamdu lillāh ḥamd al-ḥamd
Description
A treatise on the servants of God according to the different character traits (akhlāq) of the perfect human being (al-insān al-kāmil). Each servant (ʿabd Allāh) represents a spiritual modality symbolised by the association of a Divine Name and the name of a particular prophet or saint. The work is in two parts; the first consisting of 50 ʿabādila divided into five sections, the second of 67. Pablo Beneito supports Michel Chodkiewicz in relating the first 114 to the 114 suras of the Qurʾān.
Textual Criticism
OY lists 16 mss., at least seven of which can be considered historic. The best is Yusuf Aga 4859, a partial holograph with a samāʿ dated 641H. Different copies have different numbers of chapters: Ayasofia 4817 and Yusuf Aga 4859 have 117, Shehit Ali 2826 114, the Cairo printed edition (1969) 96. According to Pablo Beneito, who has made a detailed study of the text, 117 is the correct number.

كتاب الأجوبة العربية في شرح النصائح الوسفية
K. al-Ajwiba al-ʿArabiyya fī sharḥ al-naṣāʾiḥ al-Yūsufiyya(RG 9)
Composition History
Incipit
al-ḥamdu lillāh alladhī hadānā li-hadhā wa-mā kunā li-nahtadī lawlā an hadānā Allāh laqad jaʿat rusul rabbina bi-l-ḥaqq
Description
Wise sayings, accompanied by explanatory notes. IA says in the introduction that it is a collection of sayings by a certain mystic called ʿAlī al-Kurdī who was ‘smitten with divine love’, and he has appended a commentary on these sayings. Claude Addas adds: “Al-Ajwiba al-‘Arabiyya fî sharh al-nasâ’ih al-Yûsufiyya est une commentaire par Ibn ‘Arabî d’une risâla concernant les devoirs du murîd à l’égard de son shaykh rédigée par Yûsuf b. Ibrâhîm al-Shâfi’î sous l’inspiration de la rûhâniya de ‘Alî al-Kurdî, un “fou” (muwallah ) notoire de Damas (I.A. l’a rencontré, cf.,Fut., II, 522, ed. Bûlaq).”
Textual Criticism
OY lists five mss. of which two, Husein Celebi 447 and Yusuf Aga 4860, although undated, are very early, whilst Yusuf Aga 4860 is from SQ’s library, and is possibly 7th or 8th century. The internal evidence of the text – for example, the language and the mention of his wife – is strong evidence that it is by IA. In addition, the title is listed in one of the lists which IA made of his own works, Fihris muʾallafāt al-shaykh al-akbar (RG142), and it does not seem to be attributed to anyone else. Claude Addas confirms this stating that:: “L’attribution de ce texte à Ibn ‘Arabî ne fait aucun doute ; on y trouve de nombreuses références autobiographiques, par ex, l’investiture de la khirqa par ‘Abd Allâh b. Jâmī”.A number of the poems in this work appear clustered in the Paris Diwan (B.N. 2348) and seemingly nowhere else. Further information on the fully digitized text of RG 9 found in Word format at cb.rayaheen.net/userfiles/download.php?id=1395‎, which extracts as a Word document with name شرح روحانية الشيخ الكردي.doc. The text itself carries a title not listed by Osman Yahia under RG 9, namely: شرح ما نطقت به روحانية سيدي علي الكردي . JC has compared that text to Yusuf Aga 4860 and Husein Celebi 447. The 202 page text in the Word document closely matches the complete text found in Husein Celebi 447 ff.1a-149b. Decapitated Yusuf Aga 4860 p.207 – which we estimate is missing the first 14 pages (7 folios) – starts with the text found at the end of page 11 of the Word document (cf. Husein Celebi f.9a line 2). PDF versions are also available via Google (شرح روحانية الشيخ الكردي لسيدي ابن عربي.pdf).

K. al-Amr al-muḥkam(RG 28)
Damascus, 602
Composition History
In Damascus in Jumada al-ūla 602H (see Vel.51/110b)
Incipit
al-ḥamdu lillāh alladhī hadānā li-hadhā wa-mā kunā li-nahtadī lawlā an hadānā Allāh lamma qāl Allāh taʿalā li-nabihi...
Description
The title translates as “The Firm Rule regarding the conditions necessary for people on God’s Path”. The aim of the work is to give advice to spiritual guides on the kind of language and teaching which should be offered to different types of students.
Textual Criticism
A much copied work, verified by at least six historic mss, three of them definitely copied from originals. The best are Shehit Ali 1341, written around 724H in Damascus, copied from an original by IA and certified by him, and Ayasofya 4875 (probably dated 705H). OY believed the work was written in Konya in 602H on basis of a note in Veliyuddin 51 (110b), but further inspection of the note shows that the place of writing is Damascus. (See Jane Clark and Denis McAuley, Some Notes on the Manuscript Veliyuddin 51, JMIAS, 40 (2006), pp. 101-116.)

كتاب عنقاء مغرب
K. ʿAnqāʾ mughrib(RG 30)
Almeria, 595
Composition History
Incipit
ḥamidtu ilāhī wa-l-maqāmu ʿaẓīmu
Description
Written after a vision in 595H, this work is devoted to bringing out the role of the mahdī – the “rightly guided leader” – and the Seal of the Saints (whom he identifies with Jesus), discussing both a prophetic aspect which looks to the unfolding of their functions in time, and a mystical aspect which understands them as realities which can be realised in the heart of the complete human being.
Textual Criticism
Verified by at least four historic mss. The best is Berlin Oct. 3266 (described by Gerald Elmore in Islamic Sainthood in the Fullness of Time (Leiden, 1999) pp. 197-199), which was written in 597H, and is the oldest known ms. of IA’s. Also excellent is Private 1, written in 637H by a well-known disciple, Muḥammad b. ʿAbd al-Qādir al-Anṣāri, in Damascus and checked against the original in IA’s hand; however, according to Abrar Ahmed Shahi, who has prepared a critical edition of the text, this mss contains only about 50% of the work. Another early copy, dated 707H in the Turkish catalogue (http://ktp.isam.org.tr/), is Veliyuddin 1743 ff.1-79. Another copy, dated 834H, in Milli Hk 15 ff.12b-43b, is available in digital reproduction here: https://www.yazmalar.gov.tr/detay_goster.php?k=77129 JC notes that most of the poems from ʿAnqāʾ mughrib are also found as a block – and as complete poems – in the Dīwān, p. 26-33.

Konya, 602
Composition History
Incipit
al-ḥamdu li wāhib al-ʿaql wa-mubdiʿhi
Description
Written in answer to a request from a friend and companion that he should explain the journey of ascension to the ‘lord of power’ and the return to the creatures. It describes the spiritual quest in terms of a non-stop ascension through the various levels of existence and knowledge leading to the level of human perfection.
Textual Criticism
A very commonly copied work in the early collections, verified by at least four historic mss. The best is probably Yusuf Aga 7838, which is from the SQ’s library, with a samāʿ dated 651H in Aleppo in SQ’s hand. Often confused with another treatise on retreat, K. al-Khalwa (RG255) (as OY has done with Yusuf Aga 7838 copy). Other mss listed: Dar al-Kutub: dated 602H in collection 23 maj. (http://www.manuscripts.idsc.gov.eg/Manuscript/Manuscript_OPAC/Availability.aspx?ID=5323&ResID=1) and dated 600H is in collection 31 maj. كتاب الأقطاب
K. al-Aqṭāb(RG 35)
Composition History
Incipit
al-Yathribī alladhī lā naʿta yaḍbiṭuhu
Description
The function, states and places of the saints who have the spiritual rank of the ‘pole’ (quṭb). This is a series of chapters on the aqṭāb which became part of the FM chapters 462-489, with poems up to 557 (corresponding to sifr 17 of 20 in the first recension).
Textual Criticism
OY lists only one ms, Koprulu 766, 1-146, which is an early text. It carries no date, but evidence suggests that it may date to late 7th century (the scribe is the same as that of Farghānī’s Muntahā al-madārik listed here http://41.32.191.214/cgi-bin/koha/opac-detail.pl?biblionumber=39750, dated 704H). We have found a second text in the Ulu Cami library in Bursa, but as we have not yet obtained a digital copy, cannot compare the two mss. JC notes discrepancy between the text and the added red numbers from ch. 4 onwards: from chapter “five” to chapter “sixteen” the red ink number (467-478) is one less than the corresponding FM chapter number (ch.468-479); from chapter “seventeen” to “sixty” the red ink number (479-522) is four less than the corresponding FM chapter number (ch.483-526); from chapter “sixty-one” to “ninety-one” (the last in the ms) there are no red ink numbers – and these correspond to FM ch.527-557. In summary, the text of RG 35 K. al-Aqṭāb has the appearance of a highly abbreviated version of FM ch.462-557, omitting ch.480-482 entirely. It is possible that RG 35 is an early form or summary of sifr 17 of the first recension text of FM.

كتاب الأوراد الأسبوعية
K. Awrād al-usbūʿ(RG 64)
Composition History
Incipit
bismillāh fātiḥi l-wujūd
Description
A collection of fourteen prayers, one for each night and day of the week. For a detailed structure and analysis of the prayers, see Pablo Beneito and Stephen Hirtenstein Seven Days of the Heart (Oxford, 2000), with critical edition as Prayers for the Week (Oxford, 2021).
Textual Criticism
There are no historic mss. and all the surviving texts are late, though always attributed to IA. The earliest dated copy is Carullah 1094 (939H), but Veliyuddin 1833 may be older. According to Stephen Hirtenstein and Pablo Beneito it is authentic on textual grounds, and the lack of early copies is attributed to the work being only copied for close disciples, not open diffusion. There is another relatively early manuscript of Evradü’l-Eyyami’s-Seb’a (dated 949H or 950H) in Topkapi E.H. 1072 with 42 folios according to the online catalogue at https://topkapisarayi.gov.tr/en/manuscripts

الديوان الكبير
al-Dīwān al-kabīr(RG 102)
Damascus, 634H
Composition History
Incipit
lammā badā al-sirr fī fuʾādī
Description
Ibn ʿArabi’s collected poems, containing more than 800 verses, compiled in the last years of his life and finished in 634H. There are are several different versions of the work, including a six volume holograph of which we do not, at the moment, have a complete copy. Research is therefore ongoing to produce a definitive version of the whole text. The best-known version is the printed edition produced by Bulaq, which only reproduces one volume. Textual Criticism
OY lists 28 mss, of which at least four are historic. The best is Khalili 225, which is volume (juzʾ) 4 of the holograph, dated 634H in Damascus. The other holographs, Yusuf Aga 5502 and 5503, were stolen without being previously microfilmed, so there is no access to the texts, although it is believed that Uni 1438 is a copy of the first part.

فصوص الحكم
Fuṣūṣ al-ḥikam(RG 150)
Damascus, 627H
Composition History
Incipit
al-ḥamdu lillāh munzil al-ḥikam ʿalā qulūb al-kilam
Description
The most famous and widely read of all Ibn ʿArabī’s works, containing the epitome of his teachings on the meaning of the perfect human being (al-insān al-kāmil). It takes the form of 27 chapters on the prophets from Adam to Muhammad, each associated with a knowledge; for example; the wisdom on divinity in the word of Adam: the wisdom of light in the word of Joseph. A much copied and commented on work, verified by at least seven historic mss. The best is Evkaf Muzesi 1933, which is SQ’s riwāya. written in his hand in 630H, with two samāʿ in IA’s hand. Other early mss: Kastamonu Hk 2024 (710H), Paris 1340 (726H), Corum Hk 3909 (728H), Konya Ak 308 (757H)

الفتوحات المكية
al-Futūḥāt al-Makkiyya(RG 135)
Damascus, 629/636
Composition History
Incipit
al-ḥamdu lilāh alladhī awjad al-ashiyāʾ ʿan ʿadam wa-ʿadamahu wa-awqaf wajūdahā ʿalā tuwajjah kalimihi li-nataḥaqqaq bidhālik sir ḥudūthhā wa-qidamahā min qidamahi
Description
Ibn ʿArabī’s great summary of esoteric knowledge, consisting of 560 chapters and extending to nearly 10,000 pages in the holograph. It totalises all aspects of his work and all the esoteric knowledge of his era. The first recension, completed in Ṣafar 629H, exists only in copies, while the second, written between 632H and 636H, survives in IA’s own hand (see Evkaf Muzesi 1845+). There is still work to be done in distinguishing between the two recensions in the mss base, but considerable progress has been made by Julian Cook and Claude Addas in identifying “markers” which indicate the recension of any given ms. Their research indicates that many later mss. may be hybrids of the two recensions. For details of their work on the two recensions, see ancillary documents.
Textual Criticism
Verified by many mss. in both recensions. The best of the second recension is Evkaf Muzesi 1845+ in 37 volumes, which is the original in IA’s hand, written in 636H and carrying over 71 samāʿ.

ترجمان الأشواق
Tarjumān al-ashwāq(RG 767)
Mecca, 611
Composition History
Incipit
Al-ḥamdu lillāh al-ḥusn al-faʿʿāl al-jamīl alladhī yuhibb al-jamāl… layta shiʿrī hal darū ayyā qalbin malakū
Description
61 mystical love poems to the Divine Beauty, in the qaṣīda form of pre-Islamic poetry. According to Pablo Beneito (Qurrat al-ʿAyn, 2020), the original collection was 60 odes, to which were added first one then two poems in the preface. It was composed in 611H in Mecca, although the inspiration came in 604H in the meeting with Qurrat al-ʿAyn.
Textual Criticism
A much copied work, both on its own and with the commentary (RG116). There are no definitive historic mss. to verify the text in its own right, but there are several for the commentary, which includes the text of the poems. The best copies are therefore contained within mss. of Dhakhāʾir . The best stand-alone mss. are Manisa 6596 (undated, taken from SQ’s copy), and Ragib Pasha 1453 (although very late, probably also taken from SQ’s copy). There is an issue with the preface on this work. There are two basic variants; some mss carry the same preface as the commentary, Dhakhāʾir al-alāq (RG 116. Others have a longer version which begin with a khutba and IA’s account of meeting the young girl, Niẓam, who inspired the poems in Mecca. The situation is complex, with several other variants appearing in some mss (see Jane Clark Mystical Perception and Beauty: Ibn ʿArabī’s Preface to Tarjumān al-Ashwāq in JMIAS 55, 2013).

عقلة المستوفز
ʿUqlat al-mustawfiz(RG 802)
Composition History
Incipit
al-ḥamdu lillāh alladhī iftataḥa wujūd al-siwā bi-l-arwāh al-muhayyamiyya al-makhlūqa
Description
The perfect human being (al-insān al-kāmil) and the multiple degrees of Being from the most elevated orders of the Archangels to inorganic nature (OY).
Textual Criticism
An often copied work, verified by three historic mss. The best is Yusuf Ağa 4690 (undated and incomplete, but in IA’s handwriting); also Shehit Ali 1341 (725H, said to have been taken from an original, which may have been by IA) and Ayasofya 4875 (dated 753H).

وصية في كيفية حصول المعرفة القصوى
Waṣiyya fī kayfiyyat ḥuṣūl al-maʿrifa al-quṣwā(RG 821)
Composition History
Incipit
alladhī awṣika bihi ayyuhā al-akh
Description
According to OY, deals with intuitive knowledge and how this can be obtained.
Textual Criticism
OY lists 6 mss., none of which can be considered historic. Carullah 2080 (c791H in Konya) is listed by OY as being copied from an original but we could find no specific note to this effect on the text. There are a number of works with similar titles, and there is much work to be done in distinguishing them from one another. However, there is a definite unique work listed here under RG 821, with at least four mss. (Carullah 2070, Husein Celebi 448, Milli 489 and Shehit Ali 1344) being versions of the same text. N.B. OY also lists Shehit Ali 1344 under RG 817.

كتاب الوصية
K. al-Waṣiyya(RG 820)
Composition History
Incipit
ya waladī awṣika bi-taqwā allāh
Description
Textual Criticism
OY lists only one ms., Ayasofia 376 (997H), on which the Hyderabad 1948 Rasāʾil Ibn ʿArabi is based. Julian Cook argues that this is a distinct work from RG 821 (of which RG 817 is a duplicate). He has identified that within the text there are three poems (pp. 2, 5 and 7), each of which is also found in ch. 560 of Futuhat – also known as RG 818 Waṣāyā (or even Waṣiyya). So it could well be this work is in fact a set of extracts from there. Further investigation required.

كتاب اليقين
K. al-Yaqīn(RG 834)
Hebron, 602H
Composition History
Incipit
al-ḥamdu lillāh alladhī arā Ibrāhīm malakūt al-samāʾ wa-l-arḍ
Description
On the intuitive knowledge which leads to certitude. Written in Palestine in the Mosque of Yaqīn (see Veliyuddin 1826). Shehit Ali 1341 also has a note about its composition in 602H, stating that it was written in one day on Wednesday 14th Shawwāl 602H on the way to visit the tombs of Lot and Abraham.
Textual Criticism
OY lists 27 mss., of which at least two can be considered historic. The best is Veliyuddin 1826 (c.825H, copied from an original by Ayyūb b. Badr and read before IA in 621H).
