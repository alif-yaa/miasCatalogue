Bibliographic Records

K. al-ʿAbādilah
Ibn al-ʿArabī
<638
Probably Damascus
A treatise on the servants of God according to the different character traits (akhlāq) of the perfect human being (al-insān al-kāmil). Each servant (ʿabd Allāh) represents a spiritual modality symbolised by the association of a Divine Name and the name of a particular prophet or saint. The work is in two parts; the first consisting of 50 ʿabādila divided into five sections, the second of 67. Pablo Beneito supports Michel Chodkiewicz in relating the first 114 to the 114 suras of the Qurʾān.
K. al-Ajwiba al-ʿArabiyya fī sharḥ al-naṣāʾiḥ al-Yūsufiyya
Ibn al-ʿArabī
<700
Wise sayings, accompanied by explanatory notes. IA says in the introduction that it is a collection of sayings by a certain mystic called ʿAlī al-Kurdī who was ‘smitten with divine love’, and he has appended a commentary on these sayings. Claude Addas adds: “Al-Ajwiba al-‘Arabiyya fî sharh al-nasâ’ih al-Yûsufiyya est une commentaire par Ibn ‘Arabî d’une risâla concernant les devoirs du murîd à l’égard de son shaykh rédigée par Yûsuf b. Ibrâhîm al-Shâfi’î sous l’inspiration de la rûhâniya de ‘Alî al-Kurdî, un “fou” (muwallah ) notoire de Damas (I.A. l’a rencontré, cf.,Fut., II, 522, ed. Bûlaq).”

K. al-Amr al-muḥkam
Ibn al-ʿArabī
724
Damascus
The title translates as “The Firm Rule regarding the conditions necessary for people on God’s Path”. The aim of the work is to give advice to spiritual guides on the kind of language and teaching which should be offered to different types of students.
K. ʿAnqāʾ mughrib
Ibn al-ʿArabī
597
Almeria
Written after a vision in 595H, this work is devoted to bringing out the role of the mahdī – the “rightly guided leader” – and the Seal of the Saints (whom he identifies with Jesus), discussing both a prophetic aspect which looks to the unfolding of their functions in time, and a mystical aspect which understands them as realities which can be realised in the heart of the complete human being.
R. al-Anwār (K. Asrār al-khalwa)
Ibn al-ʿArabī
651
Konya
Written in answer to a request from a friend and companion that he should explain the journey of ascension to the ‘lord of power’ and the return to the creatures. It describes the spiritual quest in terms of a non-stop ascension through the various levels of existence and knowledge leading to the level of human perfection.

Treatise on the symbolism of the letter bāʾ, the first manifestation of being and the intermediary between the One and the many. K. al-Dhakhāʾir wa-l-aʿlāq
Ibn al-ʿArabī
640
Aleppo
Commentary by IA himself on his cycle of love poetry, Tarjumān al-Ashwāq (RG 767), bringing out the esoteric meaning of the verses. As IA explains in the preface, he wrote it at the request of Badr al-Ḥabashī and Ibn Sawdakīn in Aleppo in 611H because it had incited the disapproval of some of the local ʿulamāʾ.
al-Dīwān al-kabīr
Ibn al-ʿArabī
634
Damascus
Ibn ʿArabi’s collected poems, containing more than 800 verses, compiled in the last years of his life and finished in 634H. There are are several different versions of the work, including a six volume holograph of which we do not, at the moment, have a complete copy. Research is therefore ongoing to produce a definitive version of the whole text. The best-known version is the printed edition produced by Bulaq, which only reproduces one volume. An analysis of the different versions can be found in Cook/Hirtenstein: The Great Diwān and its offspring; the collection and dispersion of Ibn ʿArabī’s poetry, in JMIAS 52, 2012.

Fuṣūṣ al-ḥikam
Ibn al-ʿArabī
630
Damascus
The most famous and widely read of all Ibn ʿArabī’s works, containing the epitome of his teachings on the meaning of the perfect human being (al-insān al-kāmil). It takes the form of 27 chapters on the prophets from Adam to Muhammad, each associated with a knowledge; for example; the wisdom on divinity in the word of Adam: the wisdom of light in the word of Joseph. Ibn ʿArabī’s intention in the Fuṣūṣ is to draw out the interior, saintly wisdom of each prophet rather than the religious law which they brought.
al-Futūḥāt al-Makkiyya
Ibn al-ʿArabī
636
Damascus
Ibn ʿArabī’s great summary of esoteric knowledge, consisting of 560 chapters and extending to nearly 10,000 pages in the holograph. It totalises all aspects of his work and all the esoteric knowledge of his era. The first recension, completed in Ṣafar 629H, exists only in copies, while the second, written between 632H and 636H, survives in IA’s own hand (see Evkaf Muzesi 1845+). There is still work to be done in distinguishing between the two recensions in the mss base, but considerable progress has been made by Julian Cook and Claude Addas in identifying “markers” which indicate the recension of any given ms. Their research indicates that many later mss. may be hybrids of the two recensions. For details of their work on the two recensions, see ancillary documents.

K. Inshāʾ al-dawāʾir
Ibn al-ʿArabī
655
One of Ibn ʿArabī’s most important minor treatises, this work deals with the fundamental premises of his metaphysics; the Divine Essence and Attributes, the human being as the intermediary between Creator and Creation, and the author’s conception of the Universal Reality (al-haqīqa al-kulliya). Built around a pronounced philosophical framework, almost in the kalām style, and copiously illustrated with diagrams and tables. (Fenton and Gloton).

K. Manzil al-manāzil
Ibn al-ʿArabī
618
An intricate treatise describing an elaborate hierarchy of ‘way-stations’ or ‘abodes’ (see Michel Chodkiewicz, Ocean, 64–67). There are 19 major manāzil, corresponding to 19 classes of Qur’anic suras, many of which are sub-divided. The work is also known as Kitāb al-fahwāniyya, a technical term designating the Divine Speech addressing the servant face to face in the presence of imagination. Here, it denotes the comprehensive way-station of basmala, which has 19 letters. The treatise ends with an account of the mystical ascent (mirʿāj). See FM I:171-180.

K. Mukhtaṣar al-Durra al-fākhira
Ibn al-ʿArabī
Autobiographical sketches of IA’s masters in al-Andalus, a companion volume to his Rūḥ al-quds (RG 639). This work was written in Andalusia, and therefore there are no extant manuscripts of the original work. However, Ibn ʿArabī rewrote a version of the text later for a friend in Damascus, and this is the text, entitled K. Mukhtaṣar al-Durra al-fākhira, that we now have. Abrar Ahmad adds: Shaykh Ibn Arabi says in the first paragraph of this Mukhtaṣar al-Durra al-Fākhira: سألني بعض من يكرم علي من إخواني بمحروسة دمشق أن أوقفه على كتاب أودعته ذكر بعض من لقيته فانتفعت به في طريق الأخرة فلم يكن عندي حاضرا عند سواله فإني تركته بالمغرب فقيدت له هذا المختصر في ذكر بعض المشيخة؟ من أهل الله المتمكنين في طريق أهل التحقيق من الصالحين العارفين رضي الله عنهم جميعهم مستعينًا بالله تعالى ولم أذكر فيه إلا من لقيته ورأيته وصحبته من رجال ونساء وكبير في السن وصغير على قدر ما تذكرت منهم في هذا الوقت. وذكرت على كل واحد منهم حكاية رأيتها منه من كلام حكمة أو خرق عادة أو موعظة و تركت منهم كثيرا أضعاف ما ذكرت. (مختصر الدرة الفاخرة - الصفحة الأولي للمخطوط)

R. al-Shaykh al-ʿArabī ilā Abī ʿAlī al-Ghazzāl
Ibn al-ʿArabī
667
A short letter to al-Ghazzāl, a disciple of the Andalusian mystic Ibn al-ʿArīf (d. 536/1141 in Marrakesh). The work is also known as R. al-Nashʾatayn (RG 533, as given in both the lists which IA made of his own works, Fihris muʾallafāt al-shaykh al-akbar (RG142) and Ijāza li-l-malik al-Muẓaffar (RG 269)). R. Rūḥ al-quds fī munāṣaḥat al-nafs
Ibn al-ʿArabī
600
Mecca
Treatise originally addressed to Ibn ʿArabī’s friend in Tunis, al-Mahdawī, describing the way in which the soul is educated and advised by the Holy Spirit through the example of previous generations, present-day masters and by direct inspiration. The middle section, describing his early education with over 70 teachers in Spain and North Africa, was translated by R W J Austin in the 1970s and published as Sufis of Andalusia (London, 1971); it has subsequently become one of the best known texts by IA in modern times.
Sharḥ Khalʿ al-naʿlayn
Ibn al-ʿArabī
640
Commentary on the work of the Andalusian mystic, Abū al-Qāsim b. Qasiy, (d. 546/1151, Marrakesh). He claimed to be the Mahdī, and organised an anti-Almoravid rebellion in the Algarve. IA claims to have met his son in 590H. IA was very critical of his ideas in this work. See Addas Quest for the Red Sulphur, (Cambridge, 1993. pp. 55-7) for more on both the original and the commentary.
K. Shawāhid al-ḥaqq fī al-qalb
Ibn al-ʿArabī
617
Damascus
Maxims, consisting of a number of short conundrums, counsels, instruction, intimations, quotations and allusions. Gerald Elmore (see ‘A Selection of texts on the theme of praise from some gnomic works by Ibn ʿArabī’ in JMIAS 23, pp. 58-85) feels that this text is closely related to K, al-Tarājim (RG 737), which is probably the ‘mother’ text, but further research is needed to ascertain which came first.
K. al-Tadbīrāt al-ilāhiyya
Ibn al-ʿArabī
<638
Mawrūr (Andalusia)
This is one of IA’s earliest works, written in four days during a visit to his friend al-Mawrūrī in the village of Moron in Andalusia. It was inspired by Sirr al-Asrār (The Secrets of Secrets) by pseudo-Aristotle concerning the nature of government, with IA adding a spiritual dimension to the discussion. In 21 chapters, it explains the correspondences between the external political hierarchy of princes and rulers, and the inner rulership of the spirit within the human being.

K. al-Tajalliyāt
Ibn al-ʿArabī
620
Aleppo
Descriptions of 109 theophanies or ‘unveilings’, including IA’s encounters and conversations in the intermediary world (barzakh) with his predecessors, such as Sahl al-Tustārī, Dhū al-Nūn, Junayd etc., which illustrate themes such as unification, perfection, generosity and compassion.

Tarjumān al-ashwāq
Ibn al-ʿArabī
<800
Mecca
61 mystical love poems to the Divine Beauty, in the qaṣīda form of pre-Islamic poetry. According to Pablo Beneito (Qurrat al-ʿAyn, 2020), the original collection was 60 odes, to which were added first one then two poems in the preface. It was composed in 611H in Mecca, although the inspiration came in 604H in the meeting with Qurrat al-ʿAyn.
