K. al-ʿAbādilah

كتاب العبادله

RG: 2

Place: Probably Damascus

Date: <626

Authentification: Holograph

Composition

Date or place of composition is not known. But it must be before 626H, as there is a samāʿ on Shehit Ali 2826 dated 22nd of Dhū al-Ḥijja 626H.

Earliest witness

<638

Full title

Kitab Kalām al ʿAbādilah fi-l haqāʾiq al-Asmā: Kitab Jam‘ kalām al-ʿAbādila: Sarḥ al-ʿAbādila: Kitab alsinat al-ʿAdādila: Kitab al-ʿIbad

Alternate titles

Incipit

al-ḥamdu lillāh ḥamd al-ḥamd

Description

A treatise on the servants of God according to the different character traits (akhlāq) of the perfect human being (al-insān al-kāmil). Each servant (ʿabd Allāh) represents a spiritual modality symbolised by the association of a Divine Name and the name of a particular prophet or saint. The work is in two parts; the first consisting of 50 ʿabādila divided into five sections, the second of 67. Pablo Beneito supports Michel Chodkiewicz in relating the first 114 to the 114 suras of the Qurʾān.

Manuscripts

OY lists 16 mss., at least seven of which can be considered historic. The best is Yusuf Aga 4859, a partial holograph with a samāʿ dated 641H. Different copies have different numbers of chapters: Ayasofia 4817 and Yusuf Aga 4859 have 117, Shehit Ali 2826 114, the Cairo printed edition (1969) 96. According to Pablo Beneito, who has made a detailed study of the text, 117 is the correct number.

K. al-Ajwiba al-ʿArabiyya fī sharḥ al-naṣāʾiḥ al-Yūsufiyya

كتاب الأجوبة العربية في شرح النصائح الوسفية

RG: 9

Place:

Date:

Composition

Earliest witness

<700

Full title

K. al-Ajwiba al-ʿarabiyya fi sharḥ al-naṣāiḥ al-yūsufiyya

Alternate titles

Incipit

al-ḥamdu lillāh alladhī hadānā li-hadhā wa-mā kunā li-nahtadī lawlā an hadānā Allāh laqad jaʿat rusul rabbina bi-l-ḥaqq

Description

Wise sayings, accompanied by explanatory notes. IA says in the introduction that it is a collection of sayings by a certain mystic called ʿAlī al-Kurdī who was ‘smitten with divine love’, and he has appended a commentary on these sayings. Claude Addas adds: “Al-Ajwiba al-‘Arabiyya fî sharh al-nasâ’ih al-Yûsufiyya est une commentaire par Ibn ‘Arabî d’une risâla concernant les devoirs du murîd à l’égard de son shaykh rédigée par Yûsuf b. Ibrâhîm al-Shâfi’î sous l’inspiration de la rûhâniya de ‘Alî al-Kurdî, un “fou” (muwallah ) notoire de Damas (I.A. l’a rencontré, cf.,Fut., II, 522, ed. Bûlaq).”

Manuscripts

OY lists five mss. of which two, Husein Celebi 447 and Yusuf Aga 4860, although undated, are very early, whilst Yusuf Aga 4860 is from SQ’s library, and is possibly 7th or 8th century. The internal evidence of the text – for example, the language and the mention of his wife – is strong evidence that it is by IA. In addition, the title is listed in one of the lists which IA made of his own works, Fihris muʾallafāt al-shaykh al-akbar (RG142), and it does not seem to be attributed to anyone else. Claude Addas confirms this stating that:: “L’attribution de ce texte à Ibn ‘Arabî ne fait aucun doute ; on y trouve de nombreuses références autobiographiques, par ex, l’investiture de la khirqa par ‘Abd Allâh b. Jâmī”.A number of the poems in this work appear clustered in the Paris Diwan (B.N. 2348) and seemingly nowhere else. Further information on the fully digitized text of RG 9 found in Word format at cb.rayaheen.net/userfiles/download.php?id=1395‎, which extracts as a Word document with name شرح روحانية الشيخ الكردي.doc. The text itself carries a title not listed by Osman Yahia under RG 9, namely: شرح ما نطقت به روحانية سيدي علي الكردي . JC has compared that text to Yusuf Aga 4860 and Husein Celebi 447. The 202 page text in the Word document closely matches the complete text found in Husein Celebi 447 ff.1a-149b. Decapitated Yusuf Aga 4860 p.207 – which we estimate is missing the first 14 pages (7 folios) – starts with the text found at the end of page 11 of the Word document (cf. Husein Celebi f.9a line 2). PDF versions are also available via Google (شرح روحانية الشيخ الكردي لسيدي ابن عربي.pdf).

K. ʿAnqāʾ mughrib

كتاب عنقاء مغرب

RG: 30

Place: Almeria

Date: 595

Authentification: Autograph

Composition

Earliest witness

597

Full title

K. ʿAnqāʾ mughrib fī maʿrifa khatm al-awliyāʾ wa shams al-maghrib

Alternate titles

Incipit

ḥamidtu ilāhī wa-l-maqāmu ʿaẓīmu

Description

Written after a vision in 595H, this work is devoted to bringing out the role of the mahdī – the “rightly guided leader” – and the Seal of the Saints (whom he identifies with Jesus), discussing both a prophetic aspect which looks to the unfolding of their functions in time, and a mystical aspect which understands them as realities which can be realised in the heart of the complete human being.

Manuscripts

Verified by at least four historic mss. The best is Berlin Oct. 3266 (described by Gerald Elmore in Islamic Sainthood in the Fullness of Time (Leiden, 1999) pp. 197-199), which was written in 597H, and is the oldest known ms. of IA’s. Also excellent is Private 1, written in 637H by a well-known disciple, Muḥammad b. ʿAbd al-Qādir al-Anṣāri, in Damascus and checked against the original in IA’s hand; however, according to Abrar Ahmed Shahi, who has prepared a critical edition of the text, this mss contains only about 50% of the work. Another early copy, dated 707H in the Turkish catalogue (http://ktp.isam.org.tr/), is Veliyuddin 1743 ff.1-79. Another copy, dated 834H, in Milli Hk 15 ff.12b-43b, is available in digital reproduction here: https://www.yazmalar.gov.tr/detay_goster.php?k=77129 JC notes that most of the poems from ʿAnqāʾ mughrib are also found as a block – and as complete poems – in the Dīwān, p. 26-33.

Alternate titles

Incipit

al-ḥamdu li wāhib al-ʿaql wa-mubdiʿhi

Description

Written in answer to a request from a friend and companion that he should explain the journey of ascension to the ‘lord of power’ and the return to the creatures. (http://www.manuscripts.idsc.gov.eg/Manuscript/Manuscript_OPAC/Availability.aspx?ID=5323&ResID=1) and dated 600H is in collection 31 maj. K. al-Aqṭāb

كتاب الأقطاب

RG: 35

Place:

Date:

Composition

Earliest witness

<638?

Full title

Alternate titles

Incipit

al-Yathribī alladhī lā naʿta yaḍbiṭuhu

Description

The function, states and places of the saints who have the spiritual rank of the ‘pole’ (quṭb). This is a series of chapters on the aqṭāb which became part of the FM chapters 462-489, with poems up to 557 (corresponding to sifr 17 of 20 in the first recension).

Manuscripts

OY lists only one ms, Koprulu 766, 1-146, which is an early text. It carries no date, but evidence suggests that it may date to late 7th century (the scribe is the same as that of Farghānī’s Muntahā al-madārik listed here http://41.32.191.214/cgi-bin/koha/opac-detail.pl?biblionumber=39750, dated 704H). We have found a second text in the Ulu Cami library in Bursa, but as we have not yet obtained a digital copy, cannot compare the two mss. JC notes discrepancy between the text and the added red numbers from ch. 4 onwards: from chapter “five” to chapter “sixteen” the red ink number (467-478) is one less than the corresponding FM chapter number (ch.468-479); from chapter “seventeen” to “sixty” the red ink number (479-522) is four less than the corresponding FM chapter number (ch.483-526); from chapter “sixty-one” to “ninety-one” (the last in the ms) there are no red ink numbers – and these correspond to FM ch.527-557. In summary, the text of RG 35 K. al-Aqṭāb has the appearance of a highly abbreviated version of FM ch.462-557, omitting ch.480-482 entirely. It is possible that RG 35 is an early form or summary of sifr 17 of the first recension text of FM.

Fuṣūṣ al-ḥikam

فصوص الحكم

RG: 150

Place: Damascus

Date: 627H

Authentification: Autograph

Composition

Earliest witness

630

Full title

Alternate titles

Incipit

al-ḥamdu lillāh munzil al-ḥikam ʿalā qulūb al-kilam

Description

The most famous and widely read of all Ibn ʿArabī’s works, containing the epitome of his teachings on the meaning of the perfect human being (al-insān al-kāmil). It takes the form of 27 chapters on the prophets from Adam to Muhammad, each associated with a knowledge; for example; the wisdom on divinity in the word of Adam: the wisdom of light in the word of Joseph.
